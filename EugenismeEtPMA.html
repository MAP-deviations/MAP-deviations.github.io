Eugenics, a consequence of MAP ?


The term of eugenics means all the practices to improve humanity by selecting the individuals who have “good” genes. Francis Galton created this term in 1883, but some years before that, Charles Darwin had already said :

“With savages, the weak in body or mind are soon eliminated; and those that survive commonly exhibit a vigorous state of health. We civilised men, on the other hand, do our utmost to check the process of elimination; we build asylums for the imbecile, the maimed, and the sick; we institute poor-laws; and our medical men exert their utmost skill to save the life of every one to the last moment. There is reason to believe that vaccination has preserved thousands, who from a weak constitution would formerly have succumbed to small-pox. Thus the weak members of civilised societies propagate their kind. No one who has attended to the breeding of domestic animals will doubt that this must be highly injurious to the race of man.”

Indeed, man selected individuals according to their genes, on animals like dogs or on plants for example. In nature, natural selection keeps only strong individuals, so they can give their DNA to their descendants. But when the technological revolution happened, the progress of hygiene and medicine allowed to reduce death rate: humans didn’t undergo natural selection anymore. In this way, lots of scientists worried about a potential degeneration of humanity.

There are two kinds of eugenics : “negative eugenics”, which means avoiding the birth and the reproduction of individuals who have “lower” genes, and “positive eugenics” which means encouraging the reproduction of “better” individuals.

The most famous example of eugenics policy is the Nazi government, who used two kinds of eugenics : on the one hand, Aryan individuals, looked upon as “better” individuals, were encouraged to have a lot of children. On the other hand, “lower” individuals were put away, sterilized and often killed.
However, some other states used eugenics : for example, in the seventies, France and Sweden sterilized lots of mentally disabled. Today, in the USA, there are still some catalogs to choose a sperm donor according to his appearance and his intelligence. But in France, sperm donation, in order to avoid that this practice becomes a market, is anonymous.

But today, the main tools of eugenics are abortion and the selection of embryos, which are the consequences of Medically Assisted Procreation (MAP): indeed, we have today lots of ways to control the development of embryos, and when a severe anomaly like Down Syndrome is detected, the parents can use a Medical Termination of Pregnancy. This kind of abortion can also be used if the pregnancy or the birth represents a danger for the mother. This is the parents’ decision, but it has to be approved by a council of doctors. Moreover, the Medical Termination of Pregnancy is closely controlled by the laws. In France, all pregnancies are watched by three scans, which can detect lots of anomalies.

Indeed, in France, those three scans are compulsary and completely repaid by the French Social Security. The first scan is made during the third month of pregnancy, to date more precisely the beginning of the pregnancy, to know the number of embryos existing, to make a first detection of genetic diseases by measuring the nuchal translucency, an area behind the embryo’s neck. A thick nuchal translucency can be the evidence of Down Syndrome, dwarfism or cardiac diseases. However, this is an unreliable clue, and some embryos which have anomalous nuchal translucency become healthful children.

The second scan is made during the fifth month, it is used to follow up the growth of the embryo and to check some prospective anomalies of organs and members. The third and last scan is also used to follow up the growth of the embryo, its weight, its size, its position, the amniotic fluid…

If, during this scans, some anomalies are detected by the doctors, the parents can make other tests to confirm or not the risk of genetic disease for their future child. Let’s take the example of the Down Syndrome:

How to diagnose a Down Syndrome ?


The trisomy is a genetic disease caused by the presence of three chromosomes instead of two in the embryo’s DNA. There are many kinds of trisomy, but the best known is trisomy twenty-one, also named as Down syndrome, which affects the twenty-first pair of chromosomes. Indeed, trisomy twenty-one is one of the uncommon trisomies which don’t always drive death in utero of the embryo, however it causes lots of disorders like cardiac diseases and a mental retardation.

The only way to diagnose a trisomy without any doubt is to make an amniocentesis, which is a puncture of amniotic fluid. Analyzing this fluid will allow us to know the embryo’s karyotype. However, this process can be dangerous for the embryo, that’s why the doctors choose to practice it only when they have serious doubts about the good health of the embryo.

The first clue is, as we have seen it before, the thickness of the nuchal translucency, but it isn’t reliable enough. However, during the second scan, some anomalies like a thick neck, a large gap between the first and the second toe or some cardiac diseases can confirm the risk of a trisomy. These observations can be completed by a Human chorionic gonadotropin (HCG) control, which is a hormone in the mother’s blood during the pregnancy; if its rate changes anormally, it can be the sign of a problem.

The reckoning of the probability of trisomy relies on all these observations, but also on other factors like if the mother smokes, her age or the antecedents in the family. The amniocentesis to confirm the risk is made from a probability of 1/250 chance in France, and 1/300 in Great Britain.


Another consequence of the Medically Assisted Procreation is the selection of embryos: during an in vitro fertilization, several embryos are cultured on a laboratory, and those which have a risk of genetic diseases will never be implanted in the mother’s uterus : this is named the preimplantation genetic diagnosis. In this way, only the “best” embryos are selected, and this eventually drives to an improvement of the human genes.

But these decisions are subjective: how to choose objectively if a life is worth being lived, in spite of a major handicap ? Moreover, some people are afraid of a deviation of this practice: by beginning to manipulate the human’s genes, how could we resist the temptation of creating a “perfect” child, exactly as we ‘d like to ?
